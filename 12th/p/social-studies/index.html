Napalm and the Vietnam War

“Suddenly I saw the fire everywhere around me,” remembers Kim Phuc, a burn victim from a napalm bomb dropped on the Trảng Bàng District, North Vietnam. Phuc, and her family, along with other villagers, and South Vietnamese soldiers had been hiding in a temple for three days. As planes flew overhead, the soldiers told the villagers to run. The temple was going to be bombed. As they ran from the temple, a pilot saw the villagers, and mistook them for enemy soldiers, dropping four napalm bombs on them. (Phuc & Burge 2013)

“Two of her cousins, ages 9 months and 3 years, died in the bombing. Phuc had burns over two-thirds of her body and was not expected to live.”.(Phuc 2013)

Over the course of the war, the U.S. army dropped nearly 400,000 tons of napalm over North Vietnam. The goal was to kill enemy troops, and burn down large areas of forest to expose the enemy. Sadly a side effect of napalm was it made it easy to kill large numbers of people including citizens, women, and children. This contributed to the more than 3 million casualties, more than half of which were Vietnamese citizens. Among many other things, this led to the chorus in an Army marching song, “Napalm sticks to kids”. (Burge 2013)(Napalm in Warm 2011)

Napalm was supplied to the Military by Dow Chemical. It is a mixture of a thickening agent and petroleum. It sticks to the skin, causing severe burns with no practical way of removing it. “Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius. Napalm generates temperatures of 800 to 1,200 degrees Celsius.”. “Napalm is the most terrible pain you can imagine”. (Phuc 2013)(History.com Staff 2009)

The effects of napalm are more than physical, they are mental and emotional as well. Forty years later, Phuc’s scars still cause her pain, and impact her self image. Trauma like this affects one on every level. For many years, Phuc doubted that she would ever find a partner, get married, and have a family. (Burge 2013)

The picture of Phuc as a child became the face of the Vietnam War. The tragedy and horror of innocent people suffering in this way shaped how America saw our participation in the war, and fueled the strong anti-Vietnam War sentiment.
